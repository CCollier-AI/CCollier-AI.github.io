# 2 steps back, 3 steps forward

In my last post, I wrote about how **reframing AI as a thinking partner** helped improve how I reasoned, but getting there wasn’t a straight line.
I know this is probably a very strange title for a second post, especially considering my first post was about how AI has helped me think more critically, but stick with it.

Like most things in life (unless you’re incredibly gifted), learning new skills takes time, effort, and usually a fair amount of failure before you see anything resembling success, whatever that may look like.
It’s often those failures that teach you the most. Or at least, that’s how the story usually goes.

You might be thinking:
> “Yes Chris, I know that, but how does that apply to AI making your thinking worse?”
---

Well, when I first started using tools like ChatGPT, I was mostly using them for novelty. Things like:

> “Make up a story with X people as characters.”
(with their permission, of course)

My team and I would have a good laugh at how *strange* and impressive it felt that you could write a few sentences and **instantly** get a coherent story back.
And, very much like Alice, I found myself tumbling down the rabbit hole that is generative AI.

I started integrating Midjourney into Discord, generating image after image after image.
I was using ChatGPT to generate code for small scripts and applications. 
At one point, I even started building a print‑on‑demand e‑commerce store where people could use a Midjourney integration to create prints for their t‑shirts.
And I had ChatGPT write *all* of the code for it.
(I actually still have all of that code somewhere.)

It just went on and on.

But what struck me most throughout all of this was that, despite having all this power to create things, I didn’t actually understand what I was asking the AI to build.

Sure, I understand lots of computing architectures. I’ve lived and breathed that world for a long time.
What I mean is that I had no real understanding of the methods the AI was using or how it was implementing things, because I was laser‑focused on one thing only:

**Getting the outcome I wanted.**

My brain had effectively switched to:

> “All I care about is getting the output.”

There was very little learning, understanding, reflection, or improvement happening.
I tested the code to make sure it worked, and when it didn’t, I asked the AI to fix it, sometimes using nothing more than:

> “Fix it.”

Now, I’ve worked with engineering teams for a long time, and I know how frustrating it is when something doesn’t work and someone asks you to “just fix it”.

Fix *what*?  
What’s broken?  
Can you give me **more context**?  
(Is there a ticket for this? 😄)

Looking back, it was like I’d regressed into a child‑like state.
Frustrated that I wasn’t getting high‑quality outputs, and quick to **assume it was the AI’s fault** for not understanding what I wanted.

How wrong I was.

It was never the AI’s fault.  

It was *mine*.

I was expecting this to be some kind of wonder‑code.
That I could write a few vague sentences and it would magically understand and create everything that was going on in my head.
And while they *can* do incredible things, **they don’t work like that.**

So in some ways, yes; in the early stages of using these tools, AI *did* make my thinking worse.
What it really highlighted, though, was a pattern.

A pattern of undesirable responses.
And the one constant in all of those patterns?

**Me.**
---
That was the moment I realised that simply changing or increasing the number of tools I used;
without first changing how I thought about using them, was always going to lead to the same frustrations.

If I genuinely wanted to get the most out of AI;

*I* had to change first.

To fundamentally rethink how *I* interacted with these tools.

Lightbulb.

Once I’d had that realisation, I started experimenting differently and slowly, the results started to improve.
---
## What I’m curious about

I’d genuinely love to hear from anyone who’s had similar experiences — whether AI has made your thinking worse, better, or just *different*.
For me, this was the point where I realised that changing tools without changing how I thought about using them was never going to work.

That realisation ended up changing everything about how I now work with AI.
